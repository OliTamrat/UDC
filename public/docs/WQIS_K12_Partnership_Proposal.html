Section 01

Executive Summary

A live, hyperlocal water quality platform — purpose-built for research and education, ready for DC and Maryland classrooms today.

The University of the District of Columbia’s Water Resources Research Institute (WRRI), directed by Dr. Tolessa Deksissa within the College of Agriculture, Urban Sustainability and Environmental Sciences (CAUSES), has developed the Water Quality Intelligence Service (WQIS) — a production-grade, real-time environmental monitoring dashboard that ingests live sensor data from USGS and EPA stations across the 176-square-mile Anacostia watershed.

This proposal outlines a partnership with DC Public Schools (DCPS) and Montgomery County Public Schools (MCPS) to integrate WQIS into middle and high school environmental science, biology, and chemistry curricula. The platform transforms abstract environmental concepts into tangible, data-driven investigations using water quality readings from the 8.7-mile river and its tributaries — waterways that students in Wards 5, 6, 7, and 8, and in Silver Spring and Burtonsville, pass every day.

Why This Matters Now
DC’s Healthy Schools Act (2014) and Maryland’s Environmental Literacy Standards (2011) both mandate environmental literacy for graduation. Yet no existing educational tool connects students to real-time, local water quality data from their own watershed. Meanwhile, the Anacostia is undergoing its most significant ecological transformation in decades: DC Water completed its 23-mile tunnel system in September 2023 to eliminate combined sewer overflows, and DOEE’s restoration programs — including 35,000 deployed mussels and multiple green infrastructure sites — are producing measurable improvements. Students should be witnessing and analyzing this recovery in real time.

Section 02

The Problem

Environmental science curricula teach water quality from textbooks — not from the river outside the classroom window.

Current environmental science instruction in DC and Maryland relies on:

  • Distant case studies — the Great Lakes, Mississippi Delta, or Chesapeake Bay at basin scale — disconnected from students’ daily experience
  • Static, aged datasets that are months or years old by the time they reach a worksheet
  • No spatial connection between classroom learning and the specific streams, stormwater outfalls, and green infrastructure sites in students’ own neighborhoods
  • No access to the professional-grade monitoring tools and visualization software that working hydrologists use
The Anacostia Gap
The Anacostia River — the 8.7-mile waterway that flows from Prince George’s and Montgomery counties through the heart of eastern DC before joining the Potomac at Buzzard Point — drains a 176-square-mile watershed that encompasses the neighborhoods where over 60% of DCPS students live. Its major tributaries — Northwest Branch, Northeast Branch, Watts Branch, Hickey Run, Pope Branch, and Sligo Creek — run through school grounds and neighborhood parks. Despite being one of the most monitored urban rivers in the mid-Atlantic, no educational platform connects its real-time USGS and EPA data to K-12 curricula.

Data Sources (All Free Federal APIs — No Cost, No Quota)

  • USGS National Water Information System (NWIS) — Real-time sensor data from 7 active gauging stations: temperature, dissolved oxygen, pH, turbidity, specific conductance, E. coli. Updated every 15–60 minutes.
  • EPA Water Quality Portal (WQP / WQX3) — Lab-analyzed results for nutrients (nitrate, phosphorus), heavy metals (lead, mercury, arsenic), and biological indicators across the Anacostia HUC-10 (02070010).
Not a Mockup — Real Federal Data
Every data point on the WQIS dashboard is traceable to a USGS or EPA source record. Students work with the same data that professional hydrologists, DC’s Department of Energy & Environment (DOEE), and EPA scientists use for regulatory compliance and watershed management. The platform ingests 11,329+ records per day via automated cron jobs.

Environmental Justice Alignment
Wards 7 and 8 — where four of our twelve monitoring stations are located — are historically underserved communities with the highest flood risk and lowest per-capita green space in DC. The Anacostia has been called “DC’s forgotten river” precisely because the communities along its banks have been overlooked. This partnership places professional-grade environmental data tools directly into the hands of students in these neighborhoods — not as charity, but as scientific training that builds real analytical skills.

Jurisdiction-Specific Requirements

  • DC Healthy Schools Act (2014) — Mandates environmental literacy as a DCPS graduation requirement. WQIS provides the only real-time, DC-specific water quality data platform for classroom use.
  • Maryland Environmental Literacy Standards (2011) — Requires students to investigate local environmental issues and propose evidence-based solutions. The Anacostia’s Northwest and Northeast branches flow through Montgomery County — our upstream USGS stations provide direct relevance.
  • AP Environmental Science — WQIS’s scenario simulator, data export, and parameter explorer align with AP Exam topics: water pollution, ecosystem services, human impact assessment, and evidence-based policy analysis.

Section 08

Implementation Timeline

Phase 1 — Summer 2026 (Complete)

Platform Preparation

Hourly real-time data ingestion operational. 9 official DC GIS GeoJSON map layers integrated (wards, watersheds, sub-watersheds, waterways, waterbodies, floodplains, green infrastructure, CSO sewersheds, MS4 sewersheds). AI Research Assistant upgraded to Gemini 2.5 Flash with unlimited access. Real-time charts and compliance gauges computing from 210,000+ USGS sensor readings. Student Mode UI and lesson plans pending K-12 partnership confirmation.

Phase 2 — Fall 2026

Pilot Program (5–8 Schools)

Launch Tier 2 partnership with 3–4 DCPS schools (Wards 5, 7, 8) and 2–3 MCPS schools (Silver Spring, Burtonsville). Host teacher professional development workshop at UDC Van Ness campus. Collect usage data and pedagogical feedback.

Phase 3 — Spring 2027

Expand, Refine, Apply for Funding

Refine curriculum based on pilot teacher feedback. Expand to 15–20 schools. Launch Tier 3 Field Station Program at 4–5 pilot schools with water testing kits. Submit first grant applications (EPA, NOAA, Chesapeake Bay Trust) with school district letters of support.

Phase 4 — Fall 2027

Regional Scale & Publication

Full DCPS/MCPS district adoption. Annual Anacostia Watershed Summit at UDC. Publish student research findings in UDC and Chesapeake Bay journals. Apply for NSF ITEST ($400K–$2M) with pilot data as evidence of measurable impact.
